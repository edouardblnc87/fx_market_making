\section{Phase 3 --- Fallback Liquidity Under HFT Competition}
\label{sec:phase3}

\subsection{Context}

By Phase~3 the exchange is no longer ours alone: a latency-advantaged
HFT has installed itself on the book, sees B and C with 50\,ms of
lag (against our 200 / 170\,ms) and quotes tighter than the A-S
prescription whenever it finds it profitable to do so. The brief
reframes the DMM role: we are not here to out-earn the HFT on
frictionless flow --- that is a losing race on the latency axis ---
but to remain the book's \emph{fallback} when the HFT pulls out.
The question this section answers is quantitative. When the HFT is
active we should earn less because we are being skimmed. When the
HFT withdraws or goes one-sided we should absorb flow that would
otherwise be unserved, and that absorption is where our DMM value
accrues. Phase~3 runs a controlled 15-day backtest that contains
both regimes and decomposes the resulting P\&L accordingly.

\subsection{What we simulate}

\paragraph{A latency-advantaged competitor.} The HFT is modelled as
a second market-making agent quoting on Exchange~A. It reads the
B and C mids with a single 50\,ms lag --- four times shorter than
ours --- which lets it refresh its quotes before our order book has
seen the same move. It quotes symmetrically at $40\%$ of the
Avellaneda--Stoikov half-spread (using its own internal
$\gamma$, $k$, $\omega$, matched to our Phase~1 defaults so the
comparison is not muddied by a different utility function), so when
it is present it is \emph{always} the tightest book on the venue.
This is the adverse-selection regime customers face in steady state
on a market that has both a DMM and HFT liquidity.

\paragraph{A four-state behaviour model.} A purely always-on HFT
would not produce interesting dynamics --- it would simply compress
the spread uniformly. Real HFTs withdraw under stress, re-post
when conditions normalise and lean one-sided when inventory or
expected toxicity warrant it. We encode this with a state machine
over four modes: \textsc{active} (both sides quoted),
\textsc{one-sided-bid} (only the bid remains; the ask is pulled
because it is stale-cheap), \textsc{one-sided-ask} (symmetric) and
\textsc{offline} (no quotes at all). Transitions are driven by, in
priority order: (i)~an exogenous schedule of scripted events,
(ii)~a recovery timer that returns an \textsc{offline} HFT to
\textsc{active} after 30\,s, (iii)~a profitability floor --- if the
expected net half-spread after fees falls below $0.3$~bps the HFT
goes \textsc{offline}, (iv)~a volatility-stress threshold on an EWMA
estimator of realised vol, and (v)~a short-horizon trend detector
that flips the HFT one-sided when the mid moves by more than
$0.4$~bps over a 5\,s window (the stale side would be too cheap
given the detected drift). These triggers are deliberately
reactive: they model an HFT that \emph{chooses} to step back when
its information advantage is not large enough to cover adverse
selection, not one that simply stops at random.

\paragraph{The realistic monthly schedule.} Layered on top of the
reactive triggers is a scheduled calendar of scripted events. For
the 15-day backtest the calendar contains eight events:
two full-outage \textsc{offline} windows (a brief 25-minute outage
at $\sim\!10\%$ of the window and a longer 2.4-hour episode at
$40\%$); two volatility-triggered \textsc{offline} blackouts at
$68\%$ and $88\%$; and four one-sided squeezes --- a 1.2-hour
\textsc{one-sided-bid} at $23\%$, a 1.6-hour \textsc{one-sided-ask}
at $57\%$, and two 2-hour one-sided events near the tail at $80\%$
and $93\%$. This is not an average-case run; Phase~3 deliberately
stresses the system so that the fallback contribution is
observable rather than buried in the noise of a typical day.

\paragraph{Everything else is held fixed.} The backtest reuses the
Phase~2 calibrated \texttt{QuoterConfig}, the same 15-day stock
path, the same client-flow seed and the same capital. The only
change relative to Phase~2 is the presence of the HFT agent. Any
difference in P\&L, inventory or fill count is therefore
attributable to competition and withdrawal, not to a different
random draw.

\subsection{Analysis}

\paragraph{Competition skims the easy flow.} The HFT absorbs roughly
a quarter of the fills we would otherwise have captured: MM fills
per day fall from 939 to 718 ($-23.5\%$) and inception-spread P\&L
drops from 5\,569~USD to 3\,510~USD ($-37\%$), faster than the
fill-count decline, because the HFT preferentially wins the fills
closest to the mid --- the ones on which our inception spread is
thinnest to begin with. Consistently, the mean quoted spread
\emph{visible on Exchange~A} tightens from 8.04~bps to 6.94~bps
(and its volatility collapses from $\sigma = 1.48$ to $0.70$
bps), because whenever the HFT is \textsc{active} its 40\%-A-S
quotes anchor the top of book. In calm conditions, the HFT wins;
this is the economically efficient outcome from a customer's
perspective.

\paragraph{One-sided regimes push inventory, not hedges.} Final EUR
inventory flips sign and more than doubles in magnitude, from
$-49\,104$ in Phase~2 to $+106\,835$ in Phase~3. The mechanism is
mechanical: when the HFT enters \textsc{one-sided-bid}, its ask is
withdrawn and ours is the only ask in the book, so customer
buy-MOs hit us until the HFT returns; our EUR inventory falls. The
symmetric regime (\textsc{one-sided-ask}) lifts it back. With four
one-sided events on the 15-day calendar we are repeatedly pushed
off neutral. Yet \emph{zero} hedge legs fire against six in
Phase~2. The path never breaches the $90\%$ delta limit --- the
calibrated $\gamma$ and $\omega$ keep the A-S inventory penalty
low enough that organic mean-reversion between one-sided windows
is preferred over paying the 2--3~bps B/C taker fee. The switch
from $325$~USD of taker fees in Phase~2 to zero in Phase~3 is the
fingerprint of this behaviour, and it is also why total fees
\emph{fall} in the HFT run despite otherwise similar maker
activity.

\paragraph{P\&L is path-dependent, but not zero.} Total MtM P\&L
collapses from $+5\,761$~USD to $+675$~USD, but this headline hides
the economics. Realised cash and unrealised valuation diverge by
roughly $\pm 121\,000$~USD each --- a large open EUR position
marked at a favourable mid offsets a large realised loss from
lifting customers during one-sided squeezes. The \emph{inception
spread} we still capture (3\,510~USD over the window,
$\approx 234$~USD/day) is the genuine DMM revenue: the part of
customer flow we intermediated when the HFT was not there to
serve it. Strip the inventory-reval leg off and that is what the
balance sheet records at month-end.
Figure~\ref{fig:p3-inv-vs-price} --- inventory overlaid on the
EUR/USD path, as produced by \texttt{ctrl\_hft.report()} in the
calibration notebook --- makes the inventory argument visible.
The two sustained inventory swings align with the
\textsc{one-sided} windows on the HFT schedule: each time the HFT
withdraws one side of its quotes, the DMM becomes the only
counterparty on that side and absorbs the flow for the duration
of the window. Between windows inventory drifts back toward
neutral without any hedge leg being triggered, consistent with
the final two rows of Table~\ref{tab:p3-vs-p2}.

\subsection{Implications for Exchange A's customers --- and for our contract}

For customers on Exchange~A the arrival of the HFT is
unambiguously good news in calm conditions: effective top-of-book
spreads tighten by roughly $14\%$ (8.04~bps $\to$ 6.94~bps mean
quoted spread) and the variance of the quoted spread is cut by
more than half. The HFT is, in effect, the low-cost liquidity
provider the exchange needs.

The tail is where our value shows up. Every one-sided and
\textsc{offline} window on the Phase~3 timeline is a moment at
which the HFT's quote disappears from one or both sides of the
book, and it is the DMM commitment to always quote both sides
that keeps the venue functional through those episodes. A DMM
contract priced on volume alone would reward the wrong behaviour
here, because volume in the HFT's preferred regime will accrue to
the HFT; what the exchange is actually buying from us is
\emph{availability at tails}. The $+3{,}510$~USD inception-spread
P\&L we earn on a path populated by tail events is an empirical
estimate of that availability premium. Designing DMM compensation
to reference availability (e.g., rebate-per-second-quoted,
penalties on one-sided events caused by the DMM) rather than
fill count is the contract-theoretic implication.
