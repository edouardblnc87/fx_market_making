\section{Market Simulator for Venues B and C}
\label{sec:market-simulator}

Exchanges $B$ and $C$ are external reference venues: we never post on
them, we only read them. Our quoting engine on $A$ consumes the best
bid, the best ask, and the best-size of each, and nothing else. We
therefore do not simulate a full limit order book on $B$ or $C$: all
the depth beyond the top of book would be detail the rest of the
system never looks at. Instead we simulate a mid-price process and
build a synthetic bid/ask/depth layer around it. This is the second of
the two options offered by the subject, and it is both cheaper to run
and sufficient for everything the market maker on $A$ has to decide.
The reduced-form choice keeps realism where it matters --- spreads
that widen under stress and depth that thins with them --- while
avoiding the combinatorial cost of simulating individual order
arrivals at two venues.

\subsection{Noised mid}
\label{sec:ms-mid}

The fundamental value $V_t$ is the GBM (or GARCH, or Heston) path
produced by the stock simulator. The observed mid on $B$ and $C$ is
$V_t$ plus microstructure noise, in the spirit of \citet{roll1984simple}:
\[
  P^{\mathrm{mid}}_{B/C}(t) = V_t + \eta \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\Delta t}\cdot Z_t,
  \quad Z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1),
\]
with $\eta = 0.1$. Each venue draws an independent $Z_t$ path, so $B$
and $C$ are noised versions of the same fundamental. One subtle
point: every spread model below estimates realized volatility from
$V_t$, not from $P^{\mathrm{mid}}$, because differencing the noised
series would double-count the microstructure variance inside the vol
estimator. The zero-mean estimator used for that (see
\texttt{compute\_rv\_zero\_mean}) is unbiased for zero-drift GBM and
lower-variance than the sample std over short windows.

\subsection{Spread regimes}
\label{sec:ms-spreads}

We benchmarked five regimes of increasing realism --- Static,
Stochastic, Adaptive, Asymmetric, and Skewed --- and kept two. We
reject Static because a price-independent width ignores volatility
entirely; the market maker is widest exactly when they should be
widest, but never adapts. Stochastic scales the width with realized
vol but never returns to the competitive floor even in calm markets,
which systematically over-charges. Asymmetric mean-reverts
realistically but treats up-moves and down-moves the same way, leaving
a latency-arbitrage gap for HFTs who, seeing the vol spike 150\,ms
before us, can infer our quote path. We deploy \textbf{Adaptive on
$C$} and \textbf{Skewed on $B$}.

\paragraph{Venue $C$ --- Adaptive.}
A symmetric half-spread proportional to how much realized vol deviates
from the long-run input $\sigma$:
\[
  h(t) \;=\; h_0 \cdot \Bigl(1 + \alpha \cdot \bigl(\widehat{RV}_t/\sigma - 1\bigr)\Bigr),
  \qquad h(t) \geq 0.1 \cdot h_0,
\]
with $h_0 = \mathrm{mid}(t) \cdot s_{\mathrm{bps}} / 10000$ and
$\widehat{RV}_t$ an EMA-smoothed rolling vol over a $120$\,s window.
The width is tight at baseline, widens on shocks, and tightens
symmetrically when calm returns. A $10\%$ floor prevents collapse near
zero. $C$ carries $25\%$ of volume --- a smaller venue where informed
flow is weaker, competition enforces a tight floor, and the symmetry
of up vs. down adjustment is not a material leak.

\paragraph{Venue $B$ --- Skewed.}
Same vol-adaptive width, plus a directional shift of the quote centre
driven by a short-horizon momentum signal. Let $\hat m_t$ be the
EMA of log-returns normalised by its steady-state noise floor; the
skew is
\[
  \delta_t \;=\; \gamma \cdot h(t) \cdot \tanh\!\bigl(\mathrm{sign}(\hat m_t)\cdot
           \max(|\hat m_t| - \tau,\, 0)\bigr),
\]
with $\tau = 1.5$ the dead-band threshold. Small random wiggles map to
$\delta_t = 0$ exactly; only sustained directional moves shift the
centre. Posted quotes become $\mathrm{ask} = \mathrm{mid} + h + \delta$
and $\mathrm{bid} = \mathrm{mid} - h + \delta$: width is unchanged,
the centre moves in the direction of the trend. $B$ carries the
dominant $75\%$ of volume, where short-horizon order-flow imbalance
predicts returns \citep{cont2014price}; a momentum-unaware venue there
would be repeatedly picked off \citep{engle2004impacts,biais1995empirical}.

\subsection{Depth}
\label{sec:ms-depth}

Depth at each step is derived deterministically from spread width, not
simulated as an independent process:
\[
  \mathrm{depth}_t \;=\; \bar D \cdot \frac{\overline{S}}{S_t},
\]
where $S_t$ is the full spread and $\overline{S}$ its session mean.
The economic story is simple: when the external market maker is
confident, they quote tight and large; when stressed, they pull size
and widen. The hedge router on $A$ reads this array with the same
$200\,\mathrm{ms}$ ($B$) / $170\,\mathrm{ms}$ ($C$) latency offset as the
mid, and caps each hedge leg at the available depth.

\subsection{One stock, two venues}
\label{sec:ms-shared}

$B$ and $C$ are two \texttt{Market} objects wrapping the same
\texttt{Stock} via \texttt{copy.deepcopy}. The fundamental path $V_t$
is shared; the noise draws and spread realisations are independent.
We deliberately do not model $B/C$ price divergence: the cross-venue
edge in this project is the latency asymmetry (HFTs see $B/C$ at
$50\,\mathrm{ms}$ while we see them at $200/170\,\mathrm{ms}$), not
price disagreement between venues.

\subsection{Validation}
\label{sec:ms-validation}

Figure~\ref{fig:spread-regimes-garch} overlays the four retained
regimes on a shared GARCH-plus-jump run --- the one fundamental where
the models visibly differentiate. The top two panels plot ask and bid
quotes for Static, Stochastic, Adaptive, and Skewed against the common
mid; the four middle panels isolate each width path with a Pearson
correlation to realized vol annotated in the title; the bottom panel
shows the skew $\delta_t$ activating around the jump under the Skewed
regime and remaining near zero elsewhere. The Static width is flat up
to the mid-scaling factor, while Stochastic, Adaptive, and Skewed all
co-move with $\widehat{RV}_t$ ($\rho \approx 1.00$, $1.00$, $0.97$),
confirming the vol-adaptive mechanism fires as intended.
