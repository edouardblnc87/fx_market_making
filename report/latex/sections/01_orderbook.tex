\section{Order Book}
\label{sec:orderbook}

The order book of exchange $A$ must show a price and a quantity at ten
levels on both sides at all times. This section describes the data
structure we chose to hold that state, how submissions and
cancellations flow through it, and the invariants of the matching
engine. The design is exercised on a realistic EUR/USD seed
(mid $=1.10$, $1$\,bp tick, ten levels per side, $1$M EUR per level).

\subsection{Data structure}
\label{sec:orderbook-ds}

Two pressures shape the design. Matching requires a
price-time-ordered view of the market-maker (MM) side at clearing
time, yet the hot path is dominated by bulk requotes --- up to ten
cancels followed by ten posts per step. Keeping the book permanently
sorted would pay on every mutation for a benefit consumed only once
per step.

We therefore split orders by origin (MM versus client) into two
hash-indexed stores and keep a price-ordered view of the MM side as a
lazy cache, rebuilt only when the matcher runs. Cancels and posts
touch only the hash stores; the MM side is sorted once per clearing
call instead of being re-ordered on every mutation. Time priority
within a price level is preserved by a counter that grows by one on
every insertion, so later orders carry higher numbers than earlier
ones.

\subsection{Submissions and cancellations}
\label{sec:orderbook-updates}

The two sides are handled asymmetrically. MM cancels and posts arrive
in bulk and never run the matcher, because passive quotes cannot
cross by construction. Client orders are inserted one at a time, and
the matcher runs immediately after each insertion.

\subsection{Matching rule}
\label{sec:orderbook-match}

Three invariants govern every clearing call. Client orders only
match against MM orders, never against each other: as the sole
designated market maker for Phases~1 and~2 we are on one side of
every trade. The fill price is always the passive MM price,
never the incoming aggressive price --- clients pay the spread we
quote, which is the only source of gross revenue. And matching
follows strict price-time priority with partial fills: the engine
walks the sorted MM side, deducts the minimum of the two sizes, logs
the match, and either retires the filled order or leaves the remnant
resting. The residual shape in Figure~\ref{fig:orderbook-depth} is a
direct visual check of this rule: the top of the book is consumed
first, deeper levels are untouched.

\subsection{Fills as events}
\label{sec:orderbook-events}

Every match is dispatched synchronously, as a fill event, to a single
registered listener. In the full simulation the listener is the
Quoter, which updates inventory, trade history, and the pending
top-up queue inside the clearing call. The Quoter therefore never
polls the book: the next quote computation already sees the post-fill
inventory, which removes staleness bugs on steps that both fill and
requote. Decoupling the book from its consumer also keeps the same
engine usable in Phase~3, where a separate diagnostic listener
records fills for the exchange-client-side liquidity analysis without
touching the Phase~1 code path.
